The Story of The Creation of the Myrmidon People in Ovid's Metamorphoses

Book VII lines 614-651

What is this story about (Context)

At this point in the Metamorphoses, Liber VII, Ovid retells the fable of the creation the the Myrmidon people. Prior to their creation, this section in the Metamorphoses follows the King of Crete, Minos, who is seeking revenge for the death of his son, Androgeus. Androgeus was murdered in Athens, and, for that, Minos is searching for allies to wage a war against Athens.

Parallel to Minos’s pursuit for allies, a great plague decimates the population in Aegina. The Prince of Aegina, Aeacus, survives this plague. Distressed by the lost of his people, Aeacus seeks his said-to-be father, Jupiter, for help. As Aeacus observes a tree, sacred to Jupiter, covered with many ants, he demands Jupiter to give him the same number of citizen as the number of ants on the tree. As Aeacus dozes off, he sees the tree shake off the ants vigorously and observes the ants, now on the ground, turn into humans.

After much defeat in achieving allies, Minos turns toward Aegina. Not only is Minos turned down by Aeacus, but by the time that he arrives in Aegina, so does Cephalus, the ambassador of Athens. Aeacus recounts to both of them the tragic plague that hit the people of Aegina, as well as the godly repopulation that he experienced. The newly populated people are the Myrmidons.

This site will specifically look at the scene in which Aeacus demands citizens from his father as well as the transformation of the ants into the Myrmidons.

Retorical Analysis

In Roman society, it is societal norm to talk to the gods politely and to not demand. In literature, authors usually use the subjunctive mood when a characters talks to the gods. Furthermore, the character also can be depicted as treating a single god as plural entity. Ovid is no exception to this; in Ovid’s Metamorphosis, Liber VIII, the story of Baucis and Philemon uses these methods of politeness when either of these two main characters are speaking to the gods. Unlike Baucis and Philemon, Aeacus is not depicted using these methods, but rather depicted in the quite opposite: he treats himself as a plural being and uses the imperative to demand what he desires from the gods. Aeacus, throughout the story, shows a lack of respect when talking to the gods.

Aeacus’s lack of respect can specifically seen when he is demanding his said-to-be father to repopulate Aegina. In line 628, Aeacus says “tū mihi dā;” “Dā” is the 2nd person singular present imperative, which makes this line very direct and commanding: “You give to mihi.” Ovid specifically adds emphasis on the “you” by adding “tū,” because it is already inclusive to “dā.” The rudeness in this line is overly evident; as seen in Baucis and Philemon, when asking the gods for a favor, the subjunctive hortatory is used, not the imperative. Furthermore, Aeacus tries to instill guilt into Jupiter by saying, “nec tē, māgne pater, nostrī pudet esse parentem.” The literal translation of this line is: “and if it does not shame you, great father, to be the father of us.” However, looking at the word placement and word choice, this response is rather snarky. Ovid places “esse parentem” (“to be father”) last to place emphasis because as you are ready the line, you do not know what may shame Jupiter. As one can see, Aeacus’s lack of politeness is evident throughout the entirety of the tale.
